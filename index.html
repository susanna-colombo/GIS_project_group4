Project Overview

Understanding air quality through land cover changes
and geospatial data

Air pollution poses one of the greatest environmental health challenges of our time, affecting the quality of life of billions of people worldwide. This project investigates the relationship between land cover changes and air quality trends in Croatia, using open geospatial data from the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS) and high-resolution land cover datasets. By combining GIS analysis with satellite-derived pollution data (NO₂, PM2.5, PM10), we explore how shifts in atmospheric pollutant concentrations between the years 2021 and 2023 affect the population across different areas of the country. Results are presented through an interactive WebGIS platform.

Pollutants of Interest

NO₂

Nitrogen Dioxide

Produced mainly by vehicle engines, power plants and industrial combustion processes. It irritates the respiratory tract, aggravates asthma and with prolonged exposure increases the risk of chronic lung disease. NO₂ also reacts with other compounds in the atmosphere to form ground-level ozone and fine particulates, making it a key driver of secondary pollution.

PM10

Coarse Particulate Matter

Particles with a diameter under 10 µm, generated by road dust, construction sites, agricultural activities and industrial processes. They penetrate the upper airways and lungs, causing respiratory irritation and aggravating conditions like bronchitis and asthma. While larger than PM2.5, they are still small enough to bypass the nose and throat's natural filtering mechanisms.

PM2.5

Fine Particulate Matter

The most dangerous of the three. Particles under 2.5 µm in diameter, produced by combustion engines, wildfires, and industrial emissions, are fine enough to penetrate deep into the lungs and even enter the bloodstream. Long-term exposure is linked to cardiovascular disease, stroke, lung cancer and premature death. The WHO estimates PM2.5 is responsible for the majority of air pollution-related deaths globally and there is no known safe level of exposure.

Region of Interest

Our study area: Croatia

Area 56,594 km²
Population ~3.9 million (2023)
Capital Zagreb
Climate zones Continental inland, Mediterranean coast
Land cover Forests, agricultural fields, urban centres, coastal zones

Croatia extends from the Pannonian Plain in the east to the Adriatic coast in the west, encompassing a wide variety of land cover types. This diversity makes it an interesting case study for analysing how land use patterns correlate with air quality variations. In particular, air pollution is generally higher in inland urban and industrial areas such as Zagreb and Slavonia, while coastal regions benefit from better atmospheric dispersion and lower pollutant concentrations.
