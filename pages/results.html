Pollutant sources

First of all, we are going to recap the main sources of the three pollutants that we are interested in, that is to say PM10, PM2.5 and NO2.

Pollutant Source
PM10
  • Road transport: Brake/tyre wear, clutch wear, and road dust resuspension;
  • Agriculture: Soil tillage, harvesting, and livestock dust;
PM2.5
  • Residential heating: Inefficient wood, biomass, and coal burning (major winter source);
  • Road transport: Diesel exhaust (especially older vehicles) and gasoline direct injection engines;
  • Industry: Coal/oil-fired power plants, waste incineration, and industrial furnaces;
  • Agriculture: Ammonia emissions from fertilizers and manure reacting with industrial gases.
NO2
  • Road transport: High-temperature combustion in internal combustion engines (especially diesel vehicles);
  • Industry: Power plants;
  • Shipping & Aviation: Port areas, shipping lanes, and airport runways.

Geography of Croatia

Now that we know a bit more about each pollutant, we can start looking at the geography of Croatia. In particular, we have dressed three maps representing:
  1. all regions of Croatia;
  2. five biggest cities of Croatia;
  3. all countries bordering Croatia


    • Istria
      A prominent peninsula in the north-west driven by high-end coastal tourism, alongside localized industrial hubs like maritime shipping and energy production.
    • Lika
      These heavily forested, sparsely populated mountain regions serve as Croatia's green lungs and separate the continental interior from the coast.
    • Slavonia
      Vast, flat eastern lowlands region that acts as Croatia’s agricultural heartland, characterized by large-scale farming and open fields vulnerable to soil dust. Composed of Virovitica, Osijek, Vukovar, Slavonksi Brod and Pozega.


  • Ranked by population, the five largest cities in Croatia are:

    1. Zagreb (≈ 767,000 inhabitants)
      The capital and largest economic center, marked by urban infrastructure, heavy transit traffic, and centralized heating networks.
    2. Split (≈ 161,000 inhabitants)
      A historic coastal city functioning as the main passenger ferry hub to the islands, experiencing severe traffic and marine congestion during summer.
    3. Rijeka (≈ 108,000 inhabitants)
      A major deep-water coastal city built into steep hills, dominated by maritime shipping andoil refining infrastructure.
    4. Osijek (≈ 84,000 inhabitants)
      The cultural and economic hub of the east, situated inside an agricultural basin heavily exposed to rural air currents and farming dust.
    5. Zadar (≈ 72,000 inhabitants)
      A coastal tourist destination and transport node that has expanded its maritime footprint via a modern, dedicated cruise and ferry port.


  • Croatia shares land borders with four countries:

    • Slovenia
      to the northwest
    • Hungary
      to the northeast
    • Bosnia & Herzegovina
      to the south
    • Slavonia
      to the east

    Since all of them are not members of the European Union, they have quite different policies regarding environment.
    Country EU Member? Environment Policy Profile
    Slovenia Yes Strongly aligned with EU Green Deal goals, prioritizing forest preservation and green transport, though winter valley topography traps local wood-heating emissions.
    Hungary Yes Complies with basic EU air directives and expanding solar energy, but relies heavily on fossil fuels, gas price caps, and intensive agriculture that elevate regional baseline particles.
    Bosnia & Herzegovina No Heavily dependent on polluting and aging coal power plants, with lax industrial regulations that generate severe transboundary winter smog.
    Serbia No Committed to the Western Balkans Green Agenda but faces major implementation delays, maintaining massive heavy industrial zones and coal plants near the Croatian border.
    Montenegro No Constitutionally defined as an "ecological state" with lower overall industrial output, but still struggles with modernizing its single coal plant and urban waste management.

    Data Sources
    Two types of CAMS data were used: hourly data for December 2023, downloaded as raw NetCDF files directly from the CAMS portal using validated reanalysis and ensemble median values; and monthly aggregated GeoTIFFs, covering all 12 months of 2021 and the remaining 11 months of 2023 for the three pollutants, provided by the course professors.

    Processing Steps

    1. Aggregating hourly data for December 2023 — The hourly data was temporally averaged across the full month to produce a single monthly mean raster per pollutant, then exported as GeoTIFF.
    2. Clipping to the area of interest — All monthly layers were clipped to the Croatian national boundary. A pixel misalignment was detected between the December 2023 raster and the other provided layers and resolved by realigning all rasters onto a common grid before clipping.
    3. Yearly aggregation and concentration classification — The 12 monthly rasters for each year were averaged to produce annual mean maps for 2021 and 2023, then reclassified into five concentration classes following EU air quality limit values.
    4. Spatiotemporal trend analysis (2021–2023) — A difference map was computed for each pollutant (2023 − 2021) to visualise how concentrations evolved over the period, highlighting areas of improvement and zones where pollution increased.
