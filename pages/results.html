Pollutant sources

First of all, we are going to recap the main sources of the three pollutants that we are interested in, that is to say PM10, PM2.5 and NO2.

Pollutant Source
PM10
  • Road transport: Brake/tyre wear, clutch wear, and road dust resuspension;
  • Agriculture: Soil tillage, harvesting, and livestock dust;
PM2.5
  • Residential heating: Inefficient wood, biomass, and coal burning (major winter source);
  • Road transport: Diesel exhaust (especially older vehicles) and gasoline direct injection engines;
  • Industry: Coal/oil-fired power plants, waste incineration, and industrial furnaces;
  • Agriculture: Ammonia emissions from fertilizers and manure reacting with industrial gases.
NO2
  • Road transport: High-temperature combustion in internal combustion engines (especially diesel vehicles);
  • Industry: Power plants;
  • Shipping & Aviation: Port areas, shipping lanes, and airport runways.

Geography of Croatia

Now that we know a bit more about each pollutant, we can start looking at the geography of Croatia.
In particular, we have dressed three maps representing:

  1. all regions of Croatia
  2. the five biggest cities of Croatia
  3. all countries bordering Croatia

    • Istria
      A prominent peninsula in the north-west driven by high-end coastal tourism, alongside localized industrial hubs like maritime shipping and energy production.
    • Lika
      These heavily forested, sparsely populated mountain regions serve as Croatia's green lungs and separate the continental interior from the coast.
    • Slavonia
      Vast, flat eastern lowlands region that acts as Croatia’s agricultural heartland, characterized by large-scale farming and open fields vulnerable to soil dust.


  • Ranked by population, the five largest cities in Croatia are:

    1. Zagreb (≈ 767,000 inhabitants)
      The capital and largest economic center, marked by urban infrastructure, heavy transit traffic, and centralized heating networks.
    2. Split (≈ 161,000 inhabitants)
      A historic coastal city functioning as the main passenger ferry hub to the islands, experiencing severe traffic and marine congestion during summer.
    3. Rijeka (≈ 108,000 inhabitants)
      A major deep-water coastal city built into steep hills, dominated by maritime shipping andoil refining infrastructure.
    4. Osijek (≈ 84,000 inhabitants)
      The cultural and economic hub of the east, situated inside an agricultural basin heavily exposed to rural air currents and farming dust.
    5. Zadar (≈ 72,000 inhabitants)
      A coastal tourist destination and transport node that has expanded its maritime footprint via a modern, dedicated cruise and ferry port.

    Since all of them are not members of the European Union, they have quite different policies regarding environment.

    Country EU Member? Environment Policy Profile
    Slovenia Yes Strongly aligned with EU Green Deal goals, prioritizing forest preservation and green transport, though winter valley topography traps local wood-heating emissions.
    Hungary Yes Complies with basic EU air directives and expanding solar energy, but relies heavily on fossil fuels, gas price caps, and intensive agriculture that elevate regional baseline particles.
    Bosnia and Herzegovina No Heavily dependent on polluting and aging coal power plants, with lax industrial regulations that generate severe transboundary winter smog.
    Serbia No Committed to the Western Balkans Green Agenda but faces major implementation delays, maintaining massive heavy industrial zones and coal plants near the Croatian border.
    Montenegro No Constitutionally defined as an "ecological state" with lower overall industrial output, but still struggles with modernizing its single coal plant and urban waste management.

Air quality — Step 2

Armed with this geographical overview, we can now classify each of the biggest cities and regions into different categories.

Location Type Category Predominant pollutant expected
Zagreb (City) Continental Urban
  • Expect high NO2 from heavy traffic;
  • Expect high PM2.5 in winter due to domestic heating and industrial zones.
Split (City) Coast Touristic
  • Expect seasonal spikes in NO2 and traffic-related PM10 during summer months.
Rijeka (City) Coast Portuarial
  • Look for NO2 and sulfur/particle peaks near the coastline and industrial bays.
Osijek (City) Continental Urban
  • Highly influenced by regional agricultural emissions (PM10/PM2.5 from dust and fertilizers);
  • Subject to transboundary pollution.
Zadar (City) Coast Touristic
  • Pollution is highly seasonal.
Istria (Region) Coast Touristic
  • Air quality is generally good;
  • Can be influenced by pollution blowing over from northern Italy's Po Valley.
Lika (Region) Mountain Rural
  • Should show the lowest NO2 levels;
  • Local valley towns might show winter PM2.5 spikes from wood heating.
Slavonia (Region) Continental Rural
  • Expect large, diffuse blankets of PM10 during harvest and tillage;
  • Expect PM2.5 from regional biomass burning.

  • Since pollutant concentrations operate on different scales and are governed by different emission sources, a direct numerical comparison between PM10, PM2.5, and NO2 is not meaningful. That is why we only can use the annual mean rasters from Step 2 to understand spatial patterns within each pollutant.

    Results & Analysis — Step 2

    PM10

    Shows a relatively uniform distribution across the country, with elevated concentrations along the Slovenian border, throughout the Slavonia plain, and in the Zagreb metropolitan area.

    PM2.5

    NO2

    Analysis — Step 3

    • PM10: Class 2 areas cover 44% of Croatian territory, concentrated around Zagreb, Osijek, and Split, consistent with the result obtained with the annual mean maps.
    • PM2.5: Class 3 areas account for 35% of the country, mirroring PM10's spatial footprint. The overlap reflects their shared emission sources: road transport and agriculture.
    • NO2: presents a fundamentally different picture, with 98% of the country in Class 1 and only 2% reaching Class 2. High-concentration zones are tightly localised around Zagreb, Split, and a little part of the eastern border with Bosnia and Herzegovina.

    Beyond the spatial dimension, understanding how concentrations evolve during the year is equally important, particularly in a country like Croatia, where seasonal heating, agricultural cycles, and summer tourism create markedly different emission profiles throughout the year.

  • For this analysis, we reuse the same processing workflow as in Step 1, but aggregate the monthly CAMS data by season rather than by full year.

    Pollutant concentrations in Croatia are strongly driven by seasonal factors. That is why we have produced four seasonal concentration maps for each pollutant:

    • Winter (December – February)
    • Spring (March – May)
    • Summer (June – August)
    • Autumn (September – November)

    To quantify the seasonal amplitude of each pollutant, we computed the “Winter – Summer” difference raster using the Raster Calculator.

    Results & Analysis — Step 1

    • Positive values (cool colours) indicate zones where winter concentrations exceed summer levels, the dominant pattern expected in continental Croatia for PM2.5 and PM10, driven by residential heating.
    • Negatives values (warm colours) show zones where summer concentrations are higher, which is expected for NO2 in coastal and port areas during peak tourist season.
    PM10

    Highest concentrations occur in winter, reflecting heating-related emissions.

    PM2.5

    Follows the same winter pattern as PM10, due to similar emission sources.

    NO2

    Peaks in summer, particularly along the coast, driven by traffic and tourism activities.

Population exposure to air pollution — Steps 7 & 8

Beyond concentration levels alone, the critical public health question is where high pollution coincides with high population density.

The bivariate maps produced in Step 7 address this directly, combining pollution class (1–5) with population density class (1–5) at the provincial level to identify the territories of greatest concern.

Analysis — Step 7

Zagreb stands out as the undisputed critical zone for all three pollutants, combining the highest pollution class with the highest population density, that is to say the worst-case bivariate category.

Beyond the capital, the maps reveal meaningful differences between pollutants.

  • PM10 and PM2.5: Eastern Croatian provinces show high pollution despite moderate population density, pointing to a diffuse rural exposure risk driven by biomass heating and agricultural dust.
  • NO2: Critical zones are tightly confined to Zagreb, while the mountainous interior consistently records the lowest bivariate class.
