Results Overview

The Results section presents the geographic distribution and figures for PM10, PM2.5, and NO₂, highlighting how each pollutant affects the Croatian territory. To better contextualize the findings, we first examine the main emission sources of each pollutant, before outlining the key geographical features of Croatia, spanning coastal, mountainous and lowland regions.

Pollutant sources

First of all, we are going to recap the main sources of the three pollutants that we are interested in, that is to say PM10, PM2.5 and NO2.

Pollutant Source
PM10
  • Road transport: Brake/tyre wear, clutch wear, and road dust resuspension;
  • Agriculture: Soil tillage, harvesting, and livestock dust;
PM2.5
  • Residential heating: Inefficient wood, biomass, and coal burning (major winter source);
  • Road transport: Diesel exhaust (especially older vehicles) and gasoline direct injection engines;
  • Industry: Coal/oil-fired power plants, waste incineration, and industrial furnaces;
  • Agriculture: Ammonia emissions from fertilizers and manure reacting with industrial gases.
NO2
  • Road transport: High-temperature combustion in internal combustion engines (especially diesel vehicles);
  • Industry: Power plants;
  • Shipping & Aviation: Port areas, shipping lanes, and airport runways.

Geography of Croatia

Now that we know a bit more about each pollutant, we can start looking at the geography of Croatia. In particular, we have address three maps representing:

  1. all regions of Croatia
  2. the five biggest cities of Croatia
  3. all countries bordering Croatia

  • Croatia's territory can be divided into three major geographical macro-areas, each with distinct landscape characteristics that may help explain the spatial patterns observed in our results.

    • Istria: A prominent peninsula in the north-west driven by high-end coastal tourism, alongside localized industrial hubs like maritime shipping and energy production.
    • Lika: These heavily forested, sparsely populated mountain regions serve as Croatia's green lungs and separate the continental interior from the coast.
    • Slavonia: Vast, flat eastern lowlands region that acts as Croatia's agricultural heartland, characterized by large-scale farming and open fields vulnerable to soil dust.

  • Croatia's five largest cities are distributed across different macro-areas of the country, each with distinct urban, economic, and geographical characteristics that are relevant to the pollution patterns observed in our results.

    1. Zagreb (≈ 767,000 inhabitants)
      The capital and largest economic center, marked by urban infrastructure, heavy transit traffic, and centralized heating networks.
    2. Split (≈ 161,000 inhabitants)
      A historic coastal city functioning as the main passenger ferry hub to the islands, experiencing severe traffic and marine congestion during summer.
    3. Rijeka (≈ 108,000 inhabitants)
      A major deep-water coastal city built into steep hills, dominated by maritime shipping and oil refining infrastructure.
    4. Osijek (≈ 84,000 inhabitants)
      The cultural and economic hub of the east, situated inside an agricultural basin heavily exposed to rural air currents and farming dust.
    5. Zadar (≈ 72,000 inhabitants)
      A coastal tourist destination and transport node that has expanded its maritime footprint via a modern, dedicated cruise and ferry port.

  • Croatia shares its borders with five countries, not all of which are members of the European Union. This difference in political and regulatory frameworks translates into notably different environmental policies, which may influence the transboundary pollution patterns observed in our results.

Land use - pollutants correlation — Steps 5 & 6

This section examines the spatial correlation between land cover changes and pollutant concentration shifts in Croatia from 2021 to 2023 to analyze how land use directly impacts air quality.

Results & Analysis: Step 5

The map above illustrates the land cover changes that occurred across Croatia between 2021 and 2023, where each color represents a transition from one cover type to another, or the absence of change. Our analysis focuses on three land cover categories particularly relevant to air quality dynamics: trees, crops, and built-up areas. For each of these categories, we identified the three main sources of gain and loss in coverage, in order to better understand which land transitions may be driving the pollution patterns observed in our results.

The percentages shown in the table represent the share of pixels that transitioned from one land cover type to another between 2021 and 2023. For each category (trees, crops, and built-up areas) all pixels were first classified as either stable (unchanged), gained, or lost. The sources of gain are then calculated as the percentage of each incoming transition relative to the total number of pixels gained and analogously for loss destinations. In other words, they answer the question: among all the pixels that became X, where did they come from? And among all the pixels that were X and disappeared, what did they turn into?

Results & Analysis: Step 6

Knowing that a land cover changed is not enough, the key question is whether that change had a measurable effect on pollutant concentrations. The binary mask isolates three spatially distinct zones: areas where the land cover remained stable (baseline), areas where it was gained, and areas where it was lost. By overlaying these zones with the net change in pollutant concentration between 2021 and 2023, the zonal statistics allow us to compare how pollution evolved differently across these three categories. The resulting bar chart therefore shows whether areas that gained a certain land cover type experienced a different pollution trend compared to areas where it disappeared, making the link between land use change and air quality variation spatially explicit and quantifiable. Our analysis focuses on three pairs chosen for their environmental relevance: PM10 and Trees, PM2.5 and Crops, and NO2 and Built Area.

  • PM10 & Trees:

    The near-identical values across all three zones suggest that tree cover change has limited direct influence on PM10 concentrations.

  • PM2.5 & Crops:

    Areas where cropland was lost experienced the highest improvement in PM2.5 concentrations.

  • NO2 & Built Area:

    Areas where built-up cover expanded between 2021 and 2023 is affected by the smallest reduction in NO2 concentrations.

Population exposure to air pollution — Steps 7 & 8

Beyond concentration levels alone, the critical public health question is where high pollution coincides with high population density. The bivariate maps produced in Step 7 address this directly, combining pollution class (1–5) with population density class (1–5) at the provincial level to identify the territories of greatest concern.

Zagreb stands out as the undisputed critical zone for all three pollutants, combining the highest pollution class with the highest population density, that is to say the worst-case bivariate category.

Beyond the capital, the maps reveal meaningful differences between pollutants.

  • PM10 and PM2.5: Eastern Croatian provinces show high pollution despite moderate population density, pointing to a diffuse rural exposure risk driven by biomass heating and agricultural dust.
  • NO2: Critical zones are tightly confined to Zagreb, while the mountainous interior consistently records the lowest bivariate class.
